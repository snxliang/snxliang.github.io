A young girl grew up in a small village at the foot of the mountains in the north. Her father, a woodcutter, cared for her tenderly, though quietly. At dawn, he set out into the mountains with his long-handled axe slung over one shoulder. At dusk, she heard his faint whistle growing nearer, faint echoes down the valley.

So still she hadn't noticed, an old man sat in the shadow of a red pine. Though her father warned against bandits and evil spirits, this man seemed neither.

She could not tell whether his eyes were open, nor that he acknowledged her presence. Only a faint smile.

"Hello?"

His eyes, clearly open now, glimmered as the moon in a lily pond.

"Hello."

"I'm sorry for disturbing you, is this your home?"

"I reside here, but it does not belong to me."

"Then whose is it?"

He thought for a while how to respond, but could not before she interrupted again:

"Are you a bandit who steal all my things?"

"No."

"Are you an evil spirit who eats small children?"

"No."

"Are you a woodcutter, like my father?"

"No, though I sometimes cut wood."

"Are you a farmer?"

"No, though I sometimes grow food."

"Are you a monk? Sometimes these strange men who look a bit like you come to our village and beg for rice."

"Ah... No," he laughed slightly, "though I wouldn't mind some rice."

He gestured to a small misshapen bowl which had only a few green pinecones in it.

"Well if you're not a woodcutter, a farmer, or a monk then what are you?"

He laughed again. "Are those all the types of men you know?"

"Um, yeah, I guess."

"Your village is awfully small my friend! But alas, I shouldn't tease you so much. The people in your village call me Red Pine, because I live under a red pine. I don't much like that name." He touched the bark of the tree and gestured to its thick roots and gnarled branches. "This red pine is red pine. I am not red pine, I sit under red pine, but I am not red pine."

"Oh, I remember now, I think I've heard of you before. They say you've lived here for nine thousand years and that you know the secret to living forever."

Person-sitting-under-red-pine brightly laughed, "Do you believe them?"

"Well, that's just what the grown-ups in the village told me."

"They say many things..."

"So if you're not a woodcutter, a farmer, a monk, or red pine, then what are you?"

"I'm sorry I'm not much good at talking. Perhaps tomorrow you'll help me practice."
